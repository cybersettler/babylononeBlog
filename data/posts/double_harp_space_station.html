Give me the place to stand, and I shall move the earth. Arquimedes, As quoted by Pappus of Alexandria, Synagoge, Book VIII (c. AD 340)

Something similar can be said about bridges, if there is a place to stand, you can build a bridge. But this is here on Earth, right? What about space? Are things that different in the vacuum that separate planetary bodies?

We should start answering this puzzle by asking yet another question: how can we make a standing point in space? Do we need it anyway? You could think that there is no need for that because, as Isaac Newton taught us, a projectile can be fired from Earth with such high speed that it would be perpetually falling to Earth without ever reaching the ground. We say that the projectile has achieved orbit. This is the case of the International Space Station (ISS), they are in orbit around Earth. That means that the ISS and the astronauts inside, are always falling, and that's why they are weightless. Is not that Earth's gravity is too weak on space, is that they never reach the ground what gives them that sensation. The astronauts experience is the same as that of the passengers of a roller coaster cart going at full speed down the hill. They feel weightless.

So why we need a standing point then? Well weightlessness in space can be a problem, specially for human health. And that is the problem that this post tries to tackle. The goal is to have an environment where humans experience a force that keeps them fixed to the ground and prevents them from drifting away. On Earth we call that force gravity. So our problem is to somehow generate gravity in space. Maybe this thought evokes some fancy mechanism described in the science fiction literature, but actually there is a very simple way to generate such force, that is, circular motion.

Centrifugal Force

A simple experiment can demonstrate the power of centrifugal force to simulate gravity. If a person holds a bucket with water while spinning fast enough, the bucket can be held in an horizontal position without spilling its content.

If you have a bucket with water and grab it by its handle spinning around, if you are fast enough, the bucket can be lifted to shoulder's height without spilling the water. The so-called centrifugal force keeps the water in the bucket pressed against the bottom. The centrifugal force will have the same direction as the radius of the circle described by the motion, it will lay in the same plane and its direction will be outwards of the circle.

Two astronauts sitting on a wall

Illustration of artificial gravity in space. Two astronauts stand each on a bucket connected by a cable. The system is rotating with uniform velocity. The centrifugal force generated by the motion, pulls them to the bottom of the bucket in a similar way as gravity would if the buckets where standing on Earth.

Now let's imagine we have two giant buckets floating in space linked by their handles with a cable. The system is spinning and in each bucket there is an astronaut. The two astronauts will experience a force that pulls them to the bottom of the bucket. If the buckets are closed and spinning at the right speed, they couldn't tell the difference from being on Earth. The centrifugal force can thus be used as artificial gravity.

So what has all this to do with bridges? The two buckets system does not look like a bridge, but it is not very useful either. Our astronauts are very limited in terms of movement and could feel claustrophobic. So we would like to build something bigger, perhaps two spaceships the size of a small jet plane linked by a cable. The spaceship's floor would have to be curved shaped to ensure that as the astronauts move, they would experience the same centrifugal force as they move through the vessel.

We still have two problems to solve: the Coriolis effect and the size of the space station. The Coriolis effect is responsible for giving weather phenomenon their rotational nature on Earth, like hurricanes and cyclones. In a rotating frame, the Coriolis effect is perceived as a force that prevents moving objects to follow a straight line. The Coriolis effect can make our astronauts dizzy, and is reduced by increasing the radius of the rotating system and reducing its angular velocity. The comfort zone lies beyond 100 m radius and below 2 rpm according to a study on rotation tolerance.

The other issue to solve is the habitable size of our space station. The problem we face as we make our rotating space capsule longer is that is harder to maintain the edges in place. As the capsule is held in a single point in the middle by a cable, the edges tend to drift out creating a bending effect of the whole structure. And now it starts to look more like a "bridge" problem.

The Ada Bridge

The Ada Bridge is a cable-stayed bridge over the Sava river in Belgrade, Serbia.

In the typical bridge you want to build a structure using two standing points. In our case we want to build on a single standing point, the cable that links our vessels. It turns out that bridges using a single standing point has been built, being the Ada Bridge in Serbia an example. The Ada bridge is what is known as a cable-stayed bridge with a single pylon. What the cable-stayed system does is that it transfers the tensions in the edge of the structure to compression forces in the pylon and horizontal compression forces in the deck.

So instead of trying to solve our space habitat problem in an original way, we could apply a solution that is known to work here on Earth. Think about it, it is the same problem, with the difference that our standing point is the cable, but otherwise is the same in practice. We have a structure hanging from a single standing point. Let's imagine we have two Ada like bridges connected by their single pylons by a sufficiently strong 2 km long cable, rotating at a speed of 1.5 rpm. The astronauts on the deck would experience a pull of 1 g, the same as on Earth. The size of the habitable area is about 300 m long, and 50 m wide for each deck, that would amount for a total of 3 hectares. Not bad, eh? I call this structure the Double Harp Space Station, and it's open sourced on Github.

Double Harp Space Station

A space station composed of two cable-stayed harp bridges connected by their pylons by a 2 km cable and rotating at 1.5 rpm. The centrifugal force experienced in the decks would be equivalent to Earth's gravity.
